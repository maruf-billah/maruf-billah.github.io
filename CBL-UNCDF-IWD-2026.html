International Women’s Day 2026 – Giving Knowledge to Gain Financial Resilience

Every year, International Women's Day reminds us that empowering women is not only a matter of equality—it is a pathway to stronger families, resilient communities, and sustainable economic growth.

The global campaign theme for International Women’s Day 2026, “Give To Gain,” highlights a powerful truth: when societies invest in women—through knowledge, opportunities, and resources—everyone benefits. At the same time, the United Nations theme for this year, “Rights. Justice. Action. For ALL Women and Girls,” calls for concrete steps to remove barriers that prevent women from enjoying equal rights and opportunities. Together, these messages send a clear signal: empowering women requires both investment and action.

Across rural Bangladesh, thousands of women manage households, raise children, support agriculture, and contribute to small family businesses. Yet many remain excluded from one of the most powerful tools for empowerment: financial knowledge. Today, financial literacy is no longer optional—it is essential.

Through the SHAFAL Project, funded by United Nations Capital Development Fund (UNCDF) and implemented in partnership with City Bank PLC, a transformative initiative is underway to bring digital financial literacy to rural communities in Chattogram, Feni, Cumilla, Munshiganj, Narsingdi, and Tangail. The goal is simple but powerful: Equip women with the knowledge and confidence to manage money, use digital financial services, and participate in household financial decisions.

Why Financial Literacy Matters for Women

In many rural households, women contribute significantly to family income and financial stability. They manage daily household budgets, savings for emergencies, children’s education, and healthcare expenses. However, limited access to financial education often prevents them from fully controlling these financial responsibilities.

When women gain financial literacy, three important transformations occur:

1

Financial Confidence

Women begin to understand savings, budgeting, mobile banking, and digital financial services.

2

Economic Participation

They become active participants in income-generating activities and financial planning.

3

Household Decision-Making Power

Perhaps most importantly, women gain a stronger voice in family financial decisions — whether it is managing expenses, saving for the future, or investing in livelihoods.

Financial literacy does not simply teach women how to use money. It teaches them how to shape their future.

Through structured training sessions, participants learn about:

  • Household financial planning
  • Savings and budgeting techniques
  • Responsible borrowing
  • Safe use of digital financial services
  • Mobile banking and digital transactions
  • Protection from financial fraud

The sessions are designed to be simple, practical, and relatable, allowing women to connect the lessons directly with their daily lives. For many participants, it is the first time they formally learn how financial systems work.

From Awareness to Financial Resilience

Financial resilience is not built overnight. It begins with small steps. A woman who learns how to budget can start saving for emergencies. A mother who understands digital payments can safely receive remittances. A farmer’s wife who learns about mobile banking can manage transactions without traveling long distances. Over time, these small steps create financial stability.

When women are financially resilient:

  • Families become more prepared for economic shocks
  • Children’s education becomes more secure
  • Household investments become smarter
  • Communities become economically stronger

Financial literacy therefore becomes more than a training—it becomes a foundation for resilience.

Changing the Narrative of Rural Women

In rural Bangladesh, women are often seen as caretakers of the household. But with the right opportunities and knowledge, they are also financial managers, entrepreneurs, and economic decision-makers. Digital financial literacy helps shift this narrative. A woman who understands financial services can:

  • Manage household finances efficiently
  • Contribute to business or agricultural planning
  • Make informed decisions about savings and investments
  • Participate confidently in family financial discussions

This shift in financial decision-making power is one of the most meaningful outcomes of the initiative. Because when women participate in financial decisions, the entire household benefits.

Long-Term Impact: Empowering the Next Generation

The impact of women’s financial literacy goes far beyond the present generation. When a woman gains financial knowledge, she does not keep it to herself. She shares it—through daily practices, family conversations, and the values she teaches her children. A financially aware mother is more likely to:

  • Encourage saving habits among children
  • Prioritize education and long-term investments
  • Teach responsible spending and financial discipline
  • Build a culture of financial planning within the household

Over time, these practices create intergenerational benefits. Children grow up observing responsible financial behavior. Families become more stable. Communities gradually develop stronger economic resilience. In this way, empowering women financially does not only improve today’s households—it shapes the financial awareness of the next generation.
